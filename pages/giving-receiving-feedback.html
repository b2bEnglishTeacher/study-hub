01 — THEORY

The Art of Professional Feedback

Feedback is essential for growth, but how you deliver and receive it makes all the difference. In English-speaking workplaces, feedback tends to be indirect and constructive — the goal is improvement, not criticism. The SBI model (Situation–Behaviour–Impact) is a widely used framework for structuring feedback clearly and professionally.

✅

Giving Positive Feedback

Be specific about what was done well and why it mattered. Vague praise like "good job" is nice but forgettable. Name the behaviour and its impact.

"Your presentation yesterday was excellent — the data visualisations really helped the client understand the ROI, and they signed the contract on the spot."
🔧

Giving Constructive Feedback

Use the SBI model: describe the Situation, the specific Behaviour you observed, and the Impact it had. Focus on behaviour, not personality. Suggest improvements.

"During yesterday's meeting (situation), I noticed you interrupted the client several times (behaviour). This made them seem uncomfortable and may have affected their trust in us (impact)."
📥

Receiving Feedback Gracefully

Listen without interrupting. Don't get defensive — even if you disagree. Thank the person, ask clarifying questions, and take time to reflect before responding.

"Thank you for sharing that — I hadn't realised it was coming across that way. Could you give me an example so I can understand better?"
🚫

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Avoid the "feedback sandwich" (positive–negative–positive) as it can feel manipulative. Don't give feedback in public, when emotions are high, or without specific examples.

Instead of: "You're great but you need to be more organised, but overall you're doing fine." → Be direct: "I've noticed some deadlines have been missed recently. Can we talk about what's going on?"

Feedback Best Practices

Give feedback privately and as soon as possible after the event.
Focus on behaviour, not character — "You were late" vs. "You're lazy".
Use "I" statements: "I noticed…" / "I felt…" rather than "You always…"
When receiving feedback, listen first — resist the urge to defend immediately.
Ask for specific examples if the feedback is vague.
Always end with a forward-looking suggestion: "Going forward, it might help to…"

Writing Exercises

Practice writing professional feedback for these workplace scenarios.

Positive Feedback

Recognise good work

A colleague stayed late to fix a critical bug before a product launch. Write specific positive feedback using the SBI model.

Constructive Feedback

Address a problem professionally

A team member has been late to the last 3 morning stand-ups. Write constructive feedback using the SBI model.

Receiving Feedback

Respond gracefully

Your manager says: "I think your emails to clients are sometimes too informal." Write a professional response.
