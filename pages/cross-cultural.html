02 — THEORY

Culture & Communication at Work

In global workplaces, colleagues come from different cultural backgrounds — each with their own communication norms. Understanding the spectrum from direct to indirect communication, individual to collective decision-making, and formal to informal relationships helps you collaborate more effectively across borders.

🗣️

Direct vs. Indirect Communication

Some cultures value direct communication (e.g., Germany, Netherlands, US) — saying exactly what you mean. Others prefer indirect styles (e.g., Japan, UK, Brazil) — using context, tone, and implication. Neither is better; being aware helps prevent misunderstandings.

Direct: "I disagree with that approach."
Indirect: "That's an interesting idea. I wonder if we might also consider another option…"
⏳

Attitudes Toward Time

In monochronic cultures (US, Germany, Japan), punctuality and schedules are sacred. In polychronic cultures (Latin America, Middle East, Southern Europe), time is more flexible and relationships take priority over strict schedules.

"In some cultures, being 5 minutes late to a meeting is disrespectful. In others, meetings start when everyone has had a chance to chat and settle in."
🤝

Hierarchy & Formality

Some cultures have flat structures where everyone uses first names and speaks freely (Nordics, Netherlands). Others are more hierarchical, where titles matter and you defer to seniority (Japan, South Korea, many African nations).

"In Sweden, you'd call your CEO by their first name. In South Korea, you'd use their title and speak more formally when addressing someone senior."
💡

Building Cultural Intelligence

Cultural intelligence (CQ) is the ability to adapt to different cultural contexts. It starts with awareness: don't assume your way is "normal". Ask questions, observe, and be willing to adapt your style while staying authentic.

"I noticed meetings here tend to start with some casual conversation. I've adapted by arriving a few minutes early so I can join in — it's helped me build stronger relationships."

Cross-Cultural Tips

Don't assume — what's polite in your culture may be rude in another.
Ask open questions like "How do meetings typically work here?" to learn norms.
Be patient with language barriers — speak clearly, avoid slang, and check for understanding.
Use inclusive language — avoid idioms or references that may not translate well.
Observe before judging — silence in a meeting might mean respect, not disinterest.
03 — VOCABULARY

Useful Words & Phrases

Cultural intelligence (CQ)
Ability to adapt to different cultural contexts
Direct communication
Saying exactly what you mean, explicitly
Indirect communication
Using context and implication rather than bluntness
Hierarchy
A system where people are ranked by authority
Inclusive language
Words that don't exclude any group of people
Monochronic
Culture focused on schedules and punctuality
Polychronic
Culture where time is flexible, relationships first
Cultural awareness
Understanding that differences exist between cultures
Stereotype
An oversimplified assumption about a group
To adapt
To adjust behaviour for a different context
Non-verbal cues
Communication through body language, tone, gestures
Lost in translation
Meaning changed or lost when moving between languages
