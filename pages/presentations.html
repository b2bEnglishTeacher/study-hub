01 — THEORY

Delivering a Great Presentation

A strong presentation has three parts: a compelling opening that hooks the audience, a clear body with well-structured points, and a memorable close with a call to action. The language you use to transition between sections and handle questions is just as important as the content itself.

🎬

Opening Strong

Grab attention in the first 30 seconds with a story, a question, a surprising statistic, or a bold statement. Then introduce yourself, your topic, and the structure of your talk.

"By the end of this decade, 50% of all jobs will require skills that don't exist yet. My name is João, and today I'd like to talk about how we can prepare our teams for this shift."
📊

Structuring the Body

Organise your content into 3 main points (the Rule of Three). Use signposting language to guide the audience: "First…", "Moving on to…", "My final point is…". Keep slides minimal and visual.

"I'll cover three key areas today. First, the current skills gap. Second, our proposed training programme. And third, the expected ROI over 12 months."
🔗

Transitions & Signposting

Smooth transitions help the audience follow your logic. Use phrases to connect sections, reference visuals, and emphasise key points. Never jump abruptly between topics.

"Now that we've looked at the problem, let's turn to the solution." / "As you can see from this chart…" / "This brings me to my next point…"
🎯

Closing & Q&A

Summarise your key points, deliver a memorable closing statement, and invite questions. Don't end with "that's all" — end with purpose and energy.

"To summarise: the skills gap is real, our plan is actionable, and the ROI speaks for itself. The question isn't whether to invest — it's how soon we can start. I'd be happy to take your questions."

Presentation Tips

Use the Rule of Three — people remember information in groups of three.
Practise out loud at least 3 times — reading silently is not rehearsing.
Slow down and pause — silence is powerful and gives the audience time to absorb.
Make eye contact with different sections of the room, not just your slides.
For Q&A, repeat the question before answering — it buys you thinking time and ensures everyone heard it.

Opening

Transitions
