02 — THEORY

Anatomy of a Professional Email

A professional email follows a clear structure. Each part has a purpose — from a concise subject line that tells the reader what to expect, to a sign-off that sets the right tone. Getting these right shows competence, respect, and clarity.

📨

Subject Line & Greeting

The subject line should be specific and action-oriented — the reader should know the purpose instantly. Greetings range from formal ("Dear Mr. Smith") to semi-formal ("Hi Sarah") depending on your relationship.

Subject: Q3 Budget Proposal — Approval Needed by Friday

Dear Ms. Johnson, / Hi David, / Good morning Team,
✍️

Opening & Purpose

State your reason for writing in the first sentence. Don't bury the purpose. Common openers include "I'm writing to…", "Following up on…", or "I'd like to request…"

"I'm writing to follow up on our conversation about the new marketing budget." / "I hope this email finds you well. I'd like to request your feedback on the attached proposal."
📝

Body & Tone

Keep paragraphs short (2–3 sentences). Use a polite but direct tone. Avoid overly casual language ("Hey!", "LOL") and overly stiff language ("Pursuant to our telephonic exchange"). Match your formality to the recipient.

"Could you please review the attached document and share your comments by Thursday? If you need more time, please let me know and we can adjust the timeline."
👋

Closing & Sign-off

End with a clear call to action or next step. Choose a sign-off that matches the tone: "Best regards" (safe default), "Kind regards" (warm), "Thanks" (casual), "Yours sincerely" (very formal).

"Please let me know if you have any questions. I look forward to hearing from you.

Best regards,
Maria Silva"

Email Do's & Don'ts

Do proofread before sending — typos undermine professionalism.
Do use "Please" and "Thank you" generously — politeness costs nothing.
Do keep it concise — busy people skim emails. Get to the point quickly.
Don't use ALL CAPS — it reads as shouting.
Don't "Reply All" unless everyone genuinely needs the information.
Don't write emotional emails — if upset, draft it, wait 24 hours, then revise.
