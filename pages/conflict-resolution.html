Conflict Resolution

Learn the language and strategies for handling workplace disagreements constructively, turning tension into productive outcomes.

01 — THEORY

Navigating Workplace Conflict

Conflict at work is inevitable — but it doesn't have to be destructive. The key is how you communicate during disagreements. English-speaking workplaces value a calm, solution-oriented approach. The goal is not to "win" but to find a resolution that respects everyone involved.

🔥

Identifying the Issue

Before addressing a conflict, clarify what the actual problem is. Separate the person from the problem. Use neutral language and avoid blaming. Focus on observable behaviour, not assumptions about intent.

"I've noticed that the project timeline keeps shifting, and I think we have different expectations about deadlines. Can we align on this?"
👂

Active Listening

Let the other person speak without interrupting. Show you're listening with phrases like "I hear you", "I understand your concern", "That makes sense". Paraphrase their point to confirm understanding.

"So if I understand correctly, your concern is that the new process is adding extra work to your team. Is that right?"
🗣️

Expressing Your Perspective

Use "I" statements to share how you feel without attacking: "I feel…", "I'm concerned that…", "From my perspective…". Avoid "you always" or "you never" — these escalate conflict.

"I feel frustrated when deadlines change without notice, because it affects my ability to plan my other commitments."
🤝

Finding a Solution Together

Focus on shared goals. Ask "How can we solve this?" rather than assigning blame. Propose compromises, agree on next steps, and follow up. Document the agreement if needed.

"What if we set a rule that any deadline changes must be communicated at least 48 hours in advance? Would that work for both of us?"

Conflict Resolution Do's & Don'ts

Do address conflicts early — don't let resentment build up.
Do have the conversation privately, not in front of the team.
Do focus on behaviour and impact, not personality or intent.
Don't use accusatory language: "You always…", "You never…", "It's your fault."
Don't raise your voice or use sarcasm — stay calm and professional.
Don't involve others unnecessarily — try to resolve it one-on-one first.
02 — VOCABULARY

Useful Words & Phrases

To address an issue
To deal with or discuss a problem
To escalate
To make a situation more intense or serious
To de-escalate
To calm down a tense situation
Common ground

Mediation

To clear the air

Grievance

04 — PRACTICE

Writing Exercises

Practise resolving these workplace conflicts using professional English.

Identify

Name the issue neutrally

A colleague keeps taking credit for your ideas in meetings. Write a neutral statement identifying the problem without blaming.

Express

Use "I" statements

Your manager has been giving you last-minute tasks that disrupt your planned work. Write how you'd express your concern.

Resolve

Propose a solution

Two team members disagree on the project approach. As team lead, write what you'd say to help them find common ground.
