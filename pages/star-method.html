01 — THEORY

What is the STAR Method?

The STAR method is a structured framework used to answer behavioural interview questions — questions that begin with phrases like "Tell me about a time when…" or "Give me an example of…". Each letter represents a step in building a clear, compelling response.

S

Situation

Set the scene. Describe the context — where you were, what was happening, and any relevant background. Keep it brief but specific enough so the interviewer understands the scenario.

"In my previous role as a project coordinator at a tech startup, our team was behind schedule on a product launch with only two weeks left before the deadline."
T

Task

Explain your responsibility. What was expected of you? What challenge or goal were you facing? This separates your role from the team's overall objective.

"I was responsible for coordinating between the design and development teams to ensure all deliverables were completed on time."
A

Action

Describe what you specifically did. Focus on your individual contribution, the steps you took, and the skills you applied. Use "I" rather than "we".

"I organised daily stand-up meetings, created a shared task tracker, and personally took over the QA process to identify blockers early."
R

Result

Share the outcome. Quantify when possible — numbers, percentages, and measurable improvements make your answer more convincing. Mention what you learned if relevant.

"We launched the product on time, and the client rated our delivery 9/10. The process I introduced was adopted as a standard for future projects."

Key Tips for Using STAR

Be specific — avoid vague or generic answers. Use real examples from your experience.
Keep the Situation and Task concise (about 20% of your answer) and spend more time on Action and Result.
Always highlight your personal contribution, not just the team's effort.
Use quantifiable results whenever possible: numbers, percentages, time saved.
Prepare 3–5 STAR stories before any interview, covering different competencies (leadership, teamwork, problem-solving, conflict).
Common question starters: "Tell me about a time…", "Describe a situation where…", "Give an example of…"
02 — VOCABULARY

Useful Words & Phrases

Behavioural question
A question asking you to describe past experiences
Competency
A skill or ability evaluated in an interview
Stakeholder
A person with interest in the outcome of a project
Deliverable
A product or result that must be completed
To take the lead
To assume responsibility and guide others
Deadline
The latest time by which something must be done
To prioritise
To decide which tasks are most important
Outcome
The final result or consequence of an action
To exceed expectations
To do better than what was anticipated
Conflict resolution
The process of solving a disagreement
Quantifiable
Able to be measured with numbers
To collaborate
To work together with others towards a goal
