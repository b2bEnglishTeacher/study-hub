02 — THEORY

Why Small Talk Matters

Small talk is the social glue of professional environments. It happens before meetings, during coffee breaks, at conferences, and in elevators. While it may seem trivial, small talk builds rapport, establishes trust, and often leads to deeper business conversations. In English-speaking cultures, it's considered essential — jumping straight into business without it can feel abrupt or rude.

🚀

Opening a Conversation

Use open-ended questions or observations to start naturally. Comment on something shared — the venue, the event, or the weather. Avoid controversial topics like politics or religion.

"This is a great venue, isn't it? Have you been to this conference before?"
🔄

Keeping It Going

Show genuine interest by asking follow-up questions. Use active listening signals like "That's interesting!", "Really?", or "Tell me more about that." Mirror the other person's energy level.

"Oh, you're based in Porto? I've heard the tech scene there is booming. What's that been like?"
🎯

Safe Topics

Stick to neutral, positive topics: the event itself, travel, the city, weekend plans, industry trends, food, or the weather. These are universally comfortable and easy to discuss.

"What did you think of the keynote this morning?" / "Have you tried any good restaurants around here?"
👋

Exiting Gracefully

Know how to end a conversation politely. Thank the person, express a desire to continue later, or introduce a reason to move on. Never just walk away.

"It was really nice chatting with you. I should go find my colleague, but let's connect on LinkedIn!"

Do's and Don'ts

Do ask open-ended questions (who, what, how) instead of yes/no questions.
Do listen more than you talk — aim for a 60/40 balance in favour of listening.
Do use the person's name — it shows you're paying attention.
Don't discuss salary, politics, religion, or personal health issues.
Don't dominate the conversation — let the other person speak.
Don't look at your phone while someone is talking to you.
