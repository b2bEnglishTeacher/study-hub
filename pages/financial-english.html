02 — THEORY

Understanding Financial Language

Whether you work in finance or not, understanding financial terminology is essential for reading reports, participating in budget meetings, and making informed business decisions.

💰

Revenue & Profit

Revenue (also called "turnover" in British English) is the total income from sales. Gross profit = revenue minus cost of goods sold (COGS). Net profit = revenue minus ALL expenses. Key phrases: "Revenue grew by...", "We posted a net profit of...", "The gross margin stands at...".

"Revenue grew by 18% year-on-year to €4.2M, while net profit reached €680K, representing a net margin of 16.2%."
📉

Costs & Expenses

Fixed costs remain the same regardless of output (rent, salaries). Variable costs change with production volume (materials, shipping). Operating expenses (OPEX) are day-to-day costs. Capital expenditure (CAPEX) is investment in long-term assets.

"Operating expenses increased by 8% due to higher staffing costs, while CAPEX remained flat at €1.2M."
📊

Balance Sheet Basics

A balance sheet shows what a company owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the owners' stake (equity). Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Key terms: "current assets" (cash, receivables), "fixed assets" (property, equipment), "accounts payable/receivable".

"Total assets stand at €12M, with current assets of €3.5M and fixed assets of €8.5M. Current liabilities total €2.1M."
🔄

Cash Flow & Liquidity

Cash flow tracks money moving in and out. Positive cash flow = more money coming in than going out. Liquidity measures the ability to pay short-term obligations. Key ratios: "current ratio", "quick ratio".

"Despite strong revenue, the company faced a cash flow shortfall in Q3 due to delayed receivables from two major clients."

Financial Communication Do's & Don'ts

Do use precise numbers and percentages when presenting financial data.
Do compare figures to benchmarks, targets, or previous periods for context.
Do explain financial terms if your audience includes non-finance colleagues.
Don't round numbers excessively — "approximately €5M" is fine; "about €5M-ish" is not.
Don't use financial jargon to impress — clarity always wins.
Don't present figures without context — a €1M profit means nothing without knowing the revenue.
03 — VOCABULARY

Useful Words & Phrases

Revenue
Total income from sales before any deductions
Net profit
Income remaining after all expenses are deducted
Gross margin
Revenue minus cost of goods sold, as a percentage
OPEX
Operating expenses; day-to-day running costs
CAPEX
Capital expenditure; investment in long-term assets
Balance sheet
A financial statement showing assets, liabilities, and equity
Cash flow
The movement of money in and out of a business
Accounts receivable
Money owed to the company by customers
Accounts payable
Money the company owes to suppliers
Liquidity
The ability to meet short-term financial obligations
Depreciation
The decrease in value of an asset over time
Fiscal year
A 12-month period used for financial reporting

Writing Exercises

Write professional financial texts for the following scenarios.

Financial Summary

Write a financial summary

Write a one-paragraph financial summary for Q3: revenue was €3.8M (up 12% YoY), COGS was €1.9M, operating expenses were €1.2M, and net profit was €700K.

Cost Analysis

Explain a cost increase

Operating expenses increased by 15% this quarter. Write a paragraph explaining the increase, attributing it to new hires (€180K), office expansion (€95K), and software licences (€45K).

Balance Sheet

Describe a balance sheet position

Write a summary of a company's financial position: total assets €25M (current €8M, fixed €17M), total liabilities €10M, shareholders' equity €15M.
