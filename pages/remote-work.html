01 — THEORY

Communicating in Remote Teams

Remote work has its own communication culture. Without hallway chats and body language, written communication becomes your primary tool. The shift from synchronous (real-time) to asynchronous (at your own time) communication requires clarity, intentionality, and new vocabulary.

⏱️

Async vs. Sync Communication

Synchronous = real-time (meetings, calls, live chat). Asynchronous = at your own pace (email, Slack messages, Loom videos, docs). Remote teams default to async to respect time zones and deep work.

"This doesn't need a meeting — I'll record a Loom and share the doc async. Feel free to leave comments by EOD Thursday."
💬

Writing for Slack & Chat

Slack messages should be concise but complete. Use threads to keep channels clean. Include context: what you need, why, and by when. Use emojis for quick reactions and status updates.

"Hey team — the client deck is ready for review. Could you leave comments in the doc by tomorrow EOD? No rush on formatting — just content feedback for now. Thanks!"
📹

Video Call Etiquette

Camera on when possible (it builds trust). Mute when not speaking. Use the chat for links and side comments. Start calls with a quick check-in: "How's everyone doing?" Signal when you're done: "I'll hand it back to you."

"Before we dive in — how's everyone doing today?" / "I'll share my screen now…" / "That's all from me — I'll hand it back to you, Maria."
📍

Status Updates & Visibility

In remote teams, over-communication is better than under-communication. Share regular status updates, set your availability in Slack, and proactively share blockers. If you're offline, say when you'll be back.

"Heads up — I'm heads down on the report until 3pm, so I'll be slow to respond on Slack. Back online after that!"

Remote Communication Tips

Default to async — only schedule meetings when real-time discussion is truly needed.
Write it down — if it's not documented, it didn't happen. Use shared docs for decisions.
Respect time zones — don't expect instant replies from people in different time zones.
Use Loom or voice messages when text feels too complex — tone and context come through better.
Set boundaries — just because you work from home doesn't mean you're always available.

Writing Exercises
