02 — THEORY

Structure of a Professional Report

Professional reports follow a clear, logical structure to communicate findings, analysis, and recommendations effectively. Each section serves a distinct purpose — from setting the context to proposing actionable next steps. Understanding this structure helps you write reports that are credible, persuasive, and easy to navigate.

📋

Executive Summary & Introduction

The executive summary is a standalone overview (typically one paragraph) that captures the report's key points for time-pressed readers. The introduction then sets the context, scope, and objectives of the report in more detail.

"This report examines Q3 sales performance across EMEA, following the board's request for a regional breakdown."
🔍

Findings & Data Presentation

Present facts objectively using clear headings, bullet points, tables, and charts. Avoid interpretation in this section — let the data speak for itself. Use phrases like "The data indicates…", "As shown in Figure 1…".

"Revenue increased by 12% year-on-year, driven primarily by growth in the DACH region."
📊

Analysis & Discussion

Interpret the findings and explain WHY things happened. Use cause-effect language such as "This can be attributed to…", "A contributing factor was…", "The correlation between X and Y suggests…".

"The decline in client retention can be attributed to delayed onboarding processes in Q2."
✅

Recommendations & Conclusion

Provide action-oriented suggestions based on the analysis. Use modal verbs to frame proposals: "We recommend that…", "It is advisable to…", "The company should consider…". The conclusion ties everything together.

"We recommend increasing the onboarding team by two FTEs to reduce churn by an estimated 15%."

Report Writing Do's & Don'ts

Do use clear headings and numbered sections for easy navigation.
Do support claims with data — numbers, percentages, and comparisons.
Do write in the third person and passive voice where appropriate ("It was found that…").
Don't include opinions without evidence — reports are fact-driven.
Don't use informal language, slang, or contractions ("don't" → "do not").
Don't bury the key message — lead with your main finding or recommendation.
03 — VOCABULARY

Useful Words & Phrases

Executive summary
A brief overview of the report's key points
Findings
The results or data discovered during research
To recommend
To suggest a course of action
Year-on-year (YoY)
Comparing data to the same period last year
To attribute to
To identify the cause of something
Benchmark
A standard for comparing performance
To outline
To describe the main features or structure
Stakeholder
A person with an interest in the outcome
Scope
The range or extent of the report's focus
Methodology
The approach or methods used in research
To conclude
To reach a final decision or summary
Actionable
Practical and able to be acted upon
